PERSONAS & STORYBOARDING

The Task

My task was straightforward. I chose a physical interface and conducted user experiments with a diverse group of people. Then, I asked them a series of non-leading questions to dig deeper into their experience and feelings about using the interface. Finally, I created two personas based on this information and crafted a storyboard for one of the fictional personas.

I chose to model this project around an unusually built pasta strainer. This product intrigued me because I believed that novices in the kitchen would be ill-equiped to successfully use it. It is quite different from the strainers that most people typically use.

The Takeaways

My intuition was correct. Users without too much experience in the kitchen had a very difficult time straining pasta with this strainer. They even reported feeling "concerned, anxious, and uncomfortable" in their interactions with the strainer.

Sam embodies the frustrations of a novice chef using the strainer

Their failure taught me a valuable lesson about design. Interfaces that depart severely from the expectations and intuition of potential users must provide ample instruction for how to use it. Otherwise, unsavvy users will be lost and abandon the interface. Simple interface avoid this problem all together.

More fundamentally, this project taught me a lot about the design process. Prior to this project, I had no clue what personas were nor had I ever storyboarded before. These tasks served as the perfect introduction to the world of design and helped me to build a base of fundamental UI/UX skills.

RESPONSIVE REDESIGN

The Task

With this project, I ventured into the pits of the internet to find a website that, simply put, could use a makeover.

Once I found my site, home to Castle Awards of East Providence, I thoroughly inspected it for usability and accessibility issues. These issues formed the basis for my final product, a new, responsive website for Castle Awards.

The key problems that I identified were that the page feels incredibly cluttered, does not contain a clear heirarchy, and finding information is challenging. Additonally, the site is not responsive to different screen sizes and most of the text on the page is actually embedded within images.

A screenshot of the web landing view of Castle Awards' original site

My first step towards building the new site was to put together a set of low fidelity wireframes that sketched out the basic design of the site across mobile, tablet and web. Then, I turned the low-fi wireframes into a series of high fidelity prototypes.

Taking what I had learned from the previous project, I tried to put forward a clean and elegant design. For example, I transformed the list of links under the header image to distinct product categories with associated images. Additionally, I cleaned up the featured product and landing page views and made them much more inviting and friendlier.

I also made sure to annotate the wireframes and prototypes extensively and construct a visual style guide. I took these steps to document my thought processes and provide a set of basic instructions for how I planned to build the site.

All that's left is to build the site!

A screenshot of the featured product and product catalog views of the new site

The Takeaways

For me, the site redesign was a prime lesson in "less is more." The original Castle Awards site was flawed in a multitude of ways, but perhaps no more so than its clutter and general disorganization. To fix this, I stripped away all of the superflous information and only kept the parts of the site that are truly important. Once again, simplicity won the day.

The project also taught me tangible skills that will serve me well throughout my design career. I can envision many scenarios in which low-fi and hi-fi prototyping may come in handy, so I'm very glad that I had the opportunity to explore those disciplines here.

ITERATIVE DESIGN

The Task

Throughout class we have learned how the iterative design process can lead to better idea generation and variety in designs. Using our newfound experience in lo-fi and hi-fi prototyping, my team and I were able to complete the iterative design process to design the interface for an emerging startup. We chose Fancy, a snack-delivery service similar to Uber Eats, featured in YCombinator's demo day.

We began the design process by sketching possible designs for the site, trying to expand our own imaginations and not be limited by how food delivery platforms traditionally look. After completing four cohesive sets of designs, we evaluated what we thought did and did not make sense to include in the final version. This was a particulary interesting task because this process forced us to grapple with fundamental questions about the user flow and relative importance of various components of the site.

Ultimately, we cobbled together sketches from different sets of designs and product a low-fi prototype based on the designs.

Our low-fi prototype

After completing the low-fi prototype, we built a hi-fi prototype. Before finalizing the hi-fi prototype, we received feedback from our peers. This was an helpful checkpoint and made us aware of some design flaws that we hadn't considered. After incorporating the feedback we finalized our hi-fi prototype.

Our hi-fi prototype

The final step of the iterative design process was collecting user feedback. We created a set of tasks for users to complete and wrote questions that asked about users' experience with the prototype and the tasks. We utilized usertesting.com to recruit test subjects. Watching users navigate our prototype was incredibly interesting and showed us where additional refinement needed to be done.

The Takeaways

Watching our thoughts transform from sketches, to low-fi, and then to hi-fi prototypes that were being used by real people was incredible to watch. I loved seeing the ideas grow and change as more thought was given to both big ideas like user flow and small details like button placement.

For me, this project also emphasized the critical role that feedback and demonstration play in design. Without the peer hi-fi review, our final prototype would have functioned far worse. Additionally, if we were to deploy a website based on the prototype, the user testing feedback absolutely must be incorporated.

The user testing feedback also drove home the idea that designers must always place their users first. We must be aware of how people think and how people will approach the application. These considerations cannot come second to aesthetics or ease of developement.

DEVELOPMENT

The Task

This project was focused on using React, a framework often used to build dynamic, interactive websites. We were tasked with building a site that contained filtering, sorting, and aggregating functionalities, so I decided to build a virtual grocery store.

In thinking about how the site should be designed, I knew that I wanted the sorting and filtering options to be clear and intuitive to use. Similarly, I wanted it to be clear when items were added to the aggregator. I felt strongly that I must obey these principles because these were some of the flaws with our iterative design prototype for Fancy.

The site with a few items in the cart

The Takeaways

This project felt particularly rewarding to work on because I felt like I could apply many of the lessons that I had learned from previous projects. For example, I focused on constructing a simple and intuitive design. I put the users' needs first and tried to imitate the experience of seeing food at the grocery store to meet customer expectations.

I also really enjoyed my hands-on experience with React. I learned how to think about a state-model, how to organize an application heirarchy, how to write callback functions, and got into the nitty-gritty of front end development. It truly was the culmination of a semesters worth of learning and personal growth.
